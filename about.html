By trade I'm a UX designer with an engineer's education — a master's in socio-technical engineering focused on human-computer interaction. I design to WCAG 2.2 AA as a baseline, not an afterthought, and I stay close to developers — the best handoff is a conversation, not a document, and I can have that conversation in Swedish, English or Hungarian (with conversational Spanish when a project calls for it).

By obsession I'm a photographer — travel and street, mostly. Both crafts train the same eye: light, timing, and the patience to wait for the moment when everything lines up.

On AI

Good UX was never only about pixels. It's about navigating complex organizations, aligning people's wants and needs, and working within boundaries and legacy systems — while always considering the end user.

The tools are changing fast, and standing still isn't an option — so I use AI every day: summarizing research, drafting copy, speeding up prototyping and exploration. It's a genuine accelerant, and learning to use it well is part of the craft now. What it doesn't replace is the human work that moves an organization forward — reading the room when a stakeholder gets defensive, earning trust, aligning teams who disagree, and untangling the politics and legacy that explain why things are the way they are.

AI is also only as good as the context it's given — a company's culture, its history, the constraints and failed experiments that shaped what exists today. That picture can't be fully handed to a tool: ask it to design an app and it averages everything it's seen, landing between the best and the worst — impressive in a minute, not yet good enough for a real product. So I lean on it wherever it helps, and stay humble about the rest. Seeing exactly why, and closing that gap, is the work.

How I Work

Every project is different. A handful of things aren't.

01

Frame before you solve

The most important work happens before anything gets designed: working out what the real problem actually is. Plenty of beautifully built products still fail — because they answered the wrong question. I'd rather sit with the problem until it's clear than rush to a solution that looks finished but solves the wrong thing.

02

Constraints are the material, not the obstacle

Budget, legacy systems, regulation, the org chart, the deadline — these aren't in the way of the design, they are the design. A lot of the work is navigating the organization itself: understanding why things are the way they are, and helping people see that design isn't a matter of taste but a way to reach a better outcome. The craft is making something good because of these constraints, not despite them.

03

The job is decisions, not deliverables

You're there to move an organization toward a good outcome, and that happens mostly through other people. The Figma file is just the by-product. What counts is the decisions you help unblock and the agreement you build to get there — not just the pixels you push.

04

Subtraction is the senior move

Anyone can add a feature. Knowing what to cut — steps, options, scope, the clever idea that doesn't earn its complexity — is the harder and more valuable skill, and it usually means convincing people that less will serve them better. The hard, useful word is no.
